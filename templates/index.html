House Price Prediction Project

Introduction

In today's fast-paced and highly competitive real estate market, making an informed decision on house pricing is more challenging than ever. This project emerged from a unique opportunity to assist a friend who, after winning a substantial lottery, sought to invest in a property. The primary challenge lay in navigating the complex housing market to submit a competitive yet reasonable bid. Our solution? Developing a sophisticated house price prediction model. This project isn't just about leveraging technology for financial gain; it's about harnessing the power of data science to make smarter, more informed real estate decisions. By merging rigorous data analysis with advanced predictive modeling, we aimed to create a tool that could demystify the complexities of real estate pricing and provide clear, actionable insights.

Our approach involved a comprehensive analysis of publicly available historical data on house prices, focusing on various factors that influence selling prices. We delved into the intricacies of the data through Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA), examining the distribution and correlation of different features with house prices. The journey led us to compare multiple regression models, including Linear Regression, LASSO, Ridge Regression, Decision Trees, and advanced techniques like XgBoost and Gradient Boosting. Ultimately, Ridge Regression emerged as the optimal model, striking a balance between complexity and predictability. The model's effectiveness is rooted in its ability to minimize overfitting, ensuring that our predictions remain robust and reliable. This project is more than a technical endeavor; it's a bridge connecting data science with real-world applications, aiming to transform how we understand and predict real estate values.

Data and Information

In the pursuit of an accurate house price prediction model, we have meticulously collected a dataset comprised of historical house price records from Kaggle. This dataset serves as the cornerstone of our analysis, with the selling price designated as the dependent variable that our model seeks to predict. Our exploratory journey into the data landscape reveals a diverse set of independent variables, categorized into six continuous and seven categorical features. The continuous variables capture the house's physical characteristics, such as its:

  1. Size (Area)
  2. The number of Bedrooms
  3. Bathrooms
  4. Stories
  5. And the extent of Parking facilities available.

Complementing these are seven categorical variables that enrich our analysis by encapsulating features like:

  1. Proximity to a Mainroad
  2. The presence of a Guestroom
  3. Basement
  4. And amenities including Water Heating
  5. Airconditioning
  6. Overall Furnishing status of the property

Each variable is integral to the nuanced understanding of house valuation, providing a multi-faceted view that informs our predictive model.

Results: Model Comparison and Final Selection

Our analysis of the data landscape revealed a diverse set of features that influence house prices. To capture the nuances of these features, we employed a variety of regression models, including Linear Regression, LASSO, Ridge Regression, Decision Trees, and advanced techniques like XgBoost and Gradient Boosting. The models were evaluated based on their accuracy and error metrics, with Ridge Regression emerging as the optimal model.

Significant Predictors

These coefficients indicate the strength and direction (positive or negative) of the relationship between each feature and the target variable (price). The coefficients for "Hot Water Heating" are the largest in magnitude, suggesting that this feature has the most drastic impact on price. Specifically, having hot water heating ('yes') is associated with a significant increase in price, while not having it ('no') is associated with a decrease.The positive coefficient for 'Area' indicates that larger areas are associated with higher prices. Similarly, a greater number of bathrooms also tends to increase the house price.

Predictive Model Performance

The 'Model Accuracies' bar chart provides a comparison of the accuracies of the various models employed in the project. Notably, Ridge Regression outperforms both Linear and Lasso Regression. This superiority is attributed to Ridge's regularization approach that manages multicollinearity within predictors, a common characteristic in real estate data. Unlike Lasso, which eliminates some variables altogether, Ridge moderates the influence of all features, retaining their presence in the model. This characteristic is particularly beneficial for datasets where every variable adds some value to the prediction. Ridge Regression's leading accuracy score is indicative of its balanced approach, effectively addressing the bias-variance trade-off, thus ensuring reliable and generalizable predictions. This makes it the model of choice for our housing price prediction, as it delivers a nuanced understanding of the impact of various factors on property values. How does this website work?

To understand this, we will have to get familiar with the following critical components:

1. Predictive Model

The task at hand involves the development of a predictive model, which is then required to be serialized and stored in the form of a pickle file. For a comprehensive understanding of the model's construction process, one can refer to the code tab available on this website. The specific model employed for this purpose is the Ridge Regression model, which is a part of the sklearn package, a widely used library in the field of machine learning.

Ridge Regression, in essence, is an evolved version of the Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression. The primary distinction lies in the application of L2 regularization to the model's coefficients. This regularization technique involves adding a penalty equivalent to the square of the magnitude of the coefficients. The primary advantage of this approach is its effectiveness in mitigating the problem of overfitting. Overfitting occurs when a model learns the detail and noise in the training data to the extent that it negatively impacts the performance of the model on new data. By applying L2 regularization, Ridge Regression ensures that the model does not become overly complex or sensitive to the specific nuances of the training data.

Furthermore, this regularization technique contributes to enhancing the model's simplicity and accuracy, particularly when it comes to making predictions on data that the model has not previously encountered, also known as 'held out' data. Simplicity in a model is desirable as it typically leads to better generalization, meaning the model will perform better on new, unseen data. The balance between fitting the training data well, while also maintaining simplicity to perform effectively on new data, is a key aspect of building a robust predictive model.
